    Section 2.2 Rings

    Guiding Questions: Guiding Questions

    In this section, we’ll seek to answer the questions:
    🔗
    • What are rings and how do they relate to fields?
      🔗
    • What are subrings, and how can we tell if a given subset of a ring is a subring?
      🔗
    🔗
    In the previous section, we observed that many familiar number systems are fields but that some are not. As we will see, these non-fields are often more structurally interesting, at least from the perspective of factorization. In this section, we explore them in more detail. We start that exploration with a rigorous definition of polynomials and their arithmetic operations.
    🔗

    Subsection 2.2.1 Integral Polynomials

    Definition 2.2.1.

    A polynomial in \(x\) with coefficients in \(\Z\) is an expression of the form
    \begin{equation*} p(x) = a_0 + a_1 x + a_2 x^2 + \cdots + a_n x^n\text{,} \end{equation*}
    where \(a_0,a_1,a_1,\ldots,a_n\in\Z\) and \(a_n\ne 0\text{.}\) We will sometimes omit the function notation and denote \(p(x)\) simply by \(p\text{.}\) We call \(n\in \N_0\) the degree of the polynomial \(p\text{,}\) denoted \(\deg(p) = n\text{,}\) and \(a_0,a_1,\ldots, a_n\) the coefficients of the polynomial. The coefficient \(a_n\) is known as the leading coefficient of \(p\text{,}\) and \(a_n x^n\) is the leading term of \(p\text{.}\) The set of all integral polynomials is:
    \begin{equation*} \Z[x] := \{a_0 + a_1 x + a_2 x + \cdots + a_n x^n\mid n\in \N_0, \ a_i\in \mathbb{Z}\}. \end{equation*}
    Lastly, two polynomials are equal if and only if they have the same coefficients.
    🔗
    🔗
    We know from our experience with polynomials in calculus and other courses that when we add two polynomials we again get a polynomial. Also when we multiply two polynomials we again get a polynomial. However, in order to show that addition and multiplication are well-defined operations on \(\Z[x]\text{,}\) they must first be defined!
    🔗
    This is easier said than done. Let’s remind ourselves with an example that addition of polynomials is combine like terms. For example:
    \begin{equation*} (2x^2+(-3)x + 1) + (3x^3 + 2x^2 + x) = 3x^3 + 4x^2 + (-2)x+1. \end{equation*}
    Let’s do this computation slightly differently:
    \begin{align*} \amp (2x^2+(-3)x + 1) + (3x^3 + 2x^2 + x)\\ =\amp (0x^3+2x^2+(-3)x + 1) + (3x^3 + 2x^2 + x+0)\\ =\amp (0+3)x^3+(2+2)x^2+(-3+1)x + (1+0)\\ =\amp 3x^3+4x^2+(-2)x + 1. \end{align*}
    Notice, that to properly combine like terms we need to artificially add 0’s. This is a mental trick we tacitly do when we compute anyways but this idea of extending a polynomial by 0 is crucial to defining the addition and multiplication operations.
    🔗

    Alternatives to Extension by 0.

    An alternative (more rigorous) way of defining addition and multiplication is to not use the variable \(x\) at all. But rather to think of polynomials as defined by their coefficients. In this case, we define a polynomial as a list of integers (indexed by \(\N_0\)): \((a_0,a_1,a_2,\ldots)\) such that only finitely many of the entries are non-zero. Or equivalently, that there exists \(N\in\N_0\) such that \(a_n=0\) for all \(n\ge N\text{.}\)
    🔗

    Definition 2.2.2.

    Given two polynomials \(p(x),q(x)\in\Z[x]\text{,}\) we define their sum \(p(x)+q(x)\in\Z[x]\) as follows. Write
    \begin{align*} p(x)\amp= a_0+a_1x+a_2x^2+\cdots + a_nx^n\\ q(x)\amp= b_0+b_1x+b_2x^2+\cdots + b_mx^m. \end{align*}
    If \(n\geq m\text{,}\) we extend \(q(x)\) by 0:
    \begin{equation*} q(x) = b_0+b_1x+b_2x^2+\cdots + b_mx^m + b_{m+1}x^{m+1}+\cdots + b_nx^n \end{equation*}
    where \(b_{m+1}=\cdots = b_n = 0\text{.}\) If \(m\geq n\text{,}\) we extend \(p(x)\) by 0:
    \begin{equation*} p(x) = a_0+a_1x+a_2x^2+\cdots + a_nx^n + a_{n+1}x^{n+1}+\cdots + a_mx^m, \end{equation*}
    where \(a_{n+1} = \cdots = a_m = 0\text{.}\) Then
    \begin{equation*} p(x)+q(x) = (a_0+b_0) + (a_1+b_1)x + (a_2+b_2)x^2 + \cdots + (a_n+b_n)x^n. \end{equation*}
    🔗
    🔗
    The extra effort we’ve put in to defining polynomial addition will simplify proofs as the following proposition shows.
    🔗

    Proposition 2.2.3.

    Addition of integral polynomials is commutative. That is, for any \(p(x),q(x)\in\Z[x]\text{,}\) we have
    \begin{equation*} p(x)+q(x) = q(x)+p(x). \end{equation*}
    🔗
    🔗

    Proof.

    Suppose \(p(x),q(x)\in\Z[x]\text{.}\) Possibly by extending by 0, we can assume
    \begin{align*} p(x)\amp= a_0+a_1x+a_2x^2+\cdots + a_nx^n\\ q(x)\amp= b_0+b_1x+b_2x^2+\cdots + b_nx^n. \end{align*}
    Then
    \begin{align*} p(x)+q(x)\amp= (a_0+a_1x+a_2x^2+\cdots + a_nx^n) \\ \amp+ (b_0+b_1x+b_2x^2+\cdots + b_nx^n)\\ \amp= (a_0+b_0)+(a_1+b_1)x+(a_2+b_2)x^2+\cdots + (a_n+b_n)x^n \\ \amp= (b_0+a_0)+(b_1+a_1)x+(b_2+a_2)x^2+\cdots + (b_n+a_n)x^n \\ \amp= (b_0+b_1x+b_2x^2+\cdots + b_nx^n) \\ \amp+ (a_0+a_1x+a_2x^2+\cdots + a_nx^n)\\ \amp = q(x)+p(x) \end{align*}
    🔗
    🔗
    Notice that in the proof of Proposition 2.2.3 we did not need to go into cases based off of if the degree of \(p(x)\) is greater than or less than the degree of \(q(x)\text{.}\)
    🔗

    Exploration 2.2.1.

    Mimic the proof of Proposition 2.2.3 to prove that addition of integral polynomials is associative.
    🔗
    🔗
    We now handle polynomial multiplication. We first recall how polynomial multiplication works with the goal of writing a definition down.
    🔗

    Example 2.2.4.

    Consider the integral polynomials \(p(x) = 1+x+2x^2+3x^3\) and \(q(x) = 3+2x+x^2\text{.}\) Let’s compute their product using distribution:
    \begin{align*} p(x)\cdot q(x)\amp=(1+x+2x^2+3x^3)\cdot(3+2x+x^2) \\ \amp= 3+2x+x^2 \\ \amp+ 3x+2x^2+x^3 \\ \amp+ 6x^2+4x^3+2x^4 \\ \amp+ 9x^3+6x^4+3x^5 \\ \amp=3+5x+7x^2+14x^3+8x^4+3x^5 \end{align*}
    🔗
    🔗
    In particular, to compute the produce we needed to be able to: compute all pairwise products between terms of \(p(x)\) and terms of \(q(x)\) and then combine like terms. The following definition will be a formalization of this and in particular is somewhat involved due to the nature of poynomial multiplication.
    🔗

    Definition 2.2.5.

    Given two polynomials \(p(x),q(x)\in\Z[x]\text{,}\) we define their sum \(p(x)+q(x)\in\Z[x]\) as follows. Write
    \begin{align*} p(x)\amp= a_0+a_1x+a_2x^2+\cdots + a_nx^n\\ q(x)\amp= b_0+b_1x+b_2x^2+\cdots + b_mx^m. \end{align*}
    Extend both \(p(x)\) and \(q(x)\) by \(0\) so that:
    \begin{equation*} p(x) = a_0+a_1x+a_2x^2+\cdots + a_nx^n + a_{n+1}x^{n+1}+\cdots + a_{n+m}x^{n+m}, \end{equation*}
    where \(a_{n+1} = \cdots = a_{n+m} = 0\text{.}\) Then
    \begin{equation*} q(x) = b_0+b_1x+b_2x^2+\cdots + b_mx^m + b_{m+1}x^{m+1}+\cdots + b_{m+n}x^{m+n} \end{equation*}
    where \(b_{m+1}=\cdots = b_{m+n} = 0\) and
    \begin{equation*} p(x)\cdot q(x) = c_0+c_1x+\cdots +c_ix^i+\cdots + c_{m+n}x^{n+m} \end{equation*}
    where
    \begin{align*} c_i \amp= \sum_{k=0}^ia_kb_{i-k}\\ \amp= a_0b_i + a_1b_{i-1}+\cdots + a_{i-1}b_1 + a_ib_0. \end{align*}
    🔗
    🔗
    As was the case with polynomial addition, this definition is aimed to make proving statements about polynomial muliplication easier.
    🔗

    Proposition 2.2.6.

    Multplication of integral polynomials is commutative. That is, for any \(p(x),q(x)\in\Z[x]\text{,}\) we have \(p(x)\cdot q(x) = q(x)\cdot p(x)\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗

    Proof.

    Take \(p(x),q(x)\in\Z[x]\) of degrees \(m,n\text{,}\) respectively. Extend both by zero so that
    \begin{align*} p(x) \amp= a_0+a_1x+\cdots +a_{m+n}x^{m+n} \\ q(x) \amp= b_0+b_1x+\cdots +b_{m+n}x^{m+n} \end{align*}
    where \(a_{m+1} = \cdots = a_{m+n} = 0\) and \(b_{n+1} = \cdots = b_{m+n} = 0\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Then
    \begin{equation*} p(x)\cdot q(x) = c_0+c_1x+\cdots + c_{m+n}x^{m+n} \end{equation*}
    where
    \begin{equation*} c_i = a_0b_i+a_1b_{i-1}+\cdots + a_{i-1}b_1+a_ib_0. \end{equation*}
    We also have
    \begin{equation*} q(x)\cdot p(x) = d_0 + d_1x + \cdots + d_{m+n}x^{m+n} \end{equation*}
    where
    \begin{equation*} d_i = b_0a_i + b_1a_{i-1}+\cdots + b_{i-1}a_1+b_ia_0. \end{equation*}
    We need to show \(c_i=d_i\text{.}\)
    🔗
    To that end, recall that addition in \(\Z\) is commutative and multiplication is commutative too:
    \begin{align*} c_i \amp= a_0b_i+a_1b_{i-1}+\cdots + a_{i-1}b_1+a_ib_0 \\ \amp= b_ia_0 + b_{i-1}a_1+\cdots + b_1a_{i-1}+b_0a_i \\ \amp= b_0a_i + b_1a_{i-1}+\cdots + b_{i-1}a_1+b_ia_0\\ \amp= d_i \end{align*}
    Since the coefficients of \(p(x)\cdot q(x)\) and \(q(x)\cdot p(x)\) agreee they are equal polynomials.
    🔗
    🔗
    The preceeding proof was necessarily long and somewhat complicated because polynomial multiplication is long and complicated. It’s also worth noting that we very much relied on arithmetic properties of \(\Z\text{,}\) particular commutative multiplication.
    🔗
    We leave our discussion of integral polynomials to introduce the main course: rings. We will then return to them as important examples or rings.
    🔗
    🔗

    Subsection 2.2.2 Rings

    Definition 2.2.7.

    A ring \(R\) is a nonempty set, together with binary operations \(+\) and \(\cdot\text{,}\) denoted \((R,+,\cdot)\text{,}\) and satisfying the following axioms.
    1. Given any \(a,b,c\in R\text{,}\) \((a+b)+c = a+(b+c)\text{.}\) (Associativity of addition)
      🔗
      🔗
    2. Given any \(a,b\in R\text{,}\) \(a+b= b+a\text{.}\) (Commutativity of addition)
      🔗
      🔗
    3. There exists an element \(0_R\in R\) such that for all \(a\in R\text{,}\) \(a+0_R = 0_R + a = a\text{.}\) (Additive identity)
      🔗
      🔗
    4. Given any \(a\in R\) there exists a \(b\in R\) such that \(a+b = b + a =0_R\text{.}\) (Additive inverses)
      🔗
      🔗
    5. Given any \(a,b,c\in R\text{,}\) \((a\cdot b)\cdot c = a\cdot (b\cdot c)\text{.}\) (Associativity of multiplication)
      🔗
      🔗
    6. There exists an element \(1_R\in R\text{,}\) such that \(a\cdot 1_R = 1_R\cdot a=a\) for all \(a\in R\text{.}\) (Multiplicative identity)
      🔗
      🔗
    7. For all \(a,b,c\in R\text{,}\) \(a\cdot (b+c) = a\cdot b + a\cdot c\text{.}\) (Left-distributive property)
      🔗
      🔗
    8. For all \(a,b,c\in R\text{,}\) \((a+b)\cdot c = a\cdot c + b\cdot c\text{.}\) (Right-distributive property)
      🔗
      🔗
    As with fields, when the ring \(R\) is clear from context, we will often write \(0\) in place of \(0_R\) and \(1\) in place of \(1_R\text{.}\) The element \(1_R\) is also referred to as the unity.
    🔗
    🔗

    Non-Unital Rings.

    Most rings of interest in modern algebra, particularly commutative algebra, have a multiplicative identity. Algebraic structures that satisfy all the ring axioms except for the multiplicative identity are called non-unital rings and are sometimes referred to as rngs (without the i).
    🔗

    Compare and contrast Definitions 2.1.6 and Definition 2.2.7. What are the similarities? What are the differences?
    🔗
    🔗
    While rings do not enjoy all the properties of fields, they are incredibly useful even in applied mathematics (see, e.g., [2.2.5.1] for one recent example).
    🔗

    Definition 2.2.8.

    A ring \(R\) is said to be commutative if, for all \(a,b\in R\text{,}\) \(ab = ba\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗

    Example 2.2.9.

    The integers form a ring. We have actually assumed all the ring axioms for the majority of this text and will continue to assume them. A proof of the ring axioms requires a deeper dive on the natural numbers and is more suited to an introduction to proof course. Nevertheless, we will continue to freely use that \(\Z\) is a commutative ring. The unity is \(1_{\mathbb{Z}} = 1\) and the additive identity is \(0_{\Z} = 0\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗

    Example 2.2.10.

    All fields are rings. Fields satisfy all eight rings axioms plus an additional two: commutative multiplication and every non-zero element has a multiplicative inverse.
    🔗
    🔗
    We now return to polynomials. First, we will define polynomials over general rings.
    🔗

    Definition 2.2.11.

    Let \(R\) be a ring. A polynomial in \(x\) with coefficients in \(R\) is an expression of the form
    \begin{equation*} p(x) = a_0 + a_1 x + a_2 x^2 + \cdots + a_n x^n\text{,} \end{equation*}
    where \(a_0,a_1,a_1,\ldots,a_n\in R\) and \(a_n\ne 0\text{.}\) We will sometimes omit the function notation and denote \(p(x)\) simply by \(p\text{.}\) The set of all polynomials over \(R\) is:
    \begin{equation*} R[x] := \{a_0 + a_1 x + a_2 x + \cdots + a_n x^n\mid n\in \N_0, \ a_i\in R\}. \end{equation*}
    The general definitions of equality, degree, terms, coefficients, extension by 0, addition, and multiplication carry over directly to polynomials over \(R\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗
    Unlike fields, rings need not be commutative. In fact, an important class of examples of non-commutative rings are matrix rings. We’ll see a first example in Example 2.2.15.

    Emmy Noether.

    In the 1920s, Emmy Noether was the first to explicitly describe the ring axioms as we know them today, and her definition of a (not-necessarily-commutative) ring has led to a great deal of interesting work in algebra, number theory, and geometry, including the (see [cross-reference to target(s) "SubSec-Nonunique-Factorization" missing or not unique] for more on the historical development of the proof of Fermat’s Last Theorem). Most modern definitions of ring agree with our Definition 2.2.7 and allow for rings with noncommutative multiplication.
    🔗
    The following theorem states that the set of polynomials with coefficients in a ring \(R\) is itself a ring under the usual operations of polynomial addition of like terms, and multiplication via distribution. The proof is not tricky, but a rigorous justification (especially of, e.g., the associativity of polynomial multiplication) is tedious, and thus is omitted.
    🔗

    Theorem 2.2.13. Theorem.

    If \(R\) is a (commutative) ring, then \(R[x]\) is a (commutative) ring.
    🔗
    🔗

    Proof.

    As in the proof of Proposition 2.2.6, we see that if \(R[x]\) is a ring and \(R\) is commutative, then \(R[x]\) must also be commutative. The proof carries over mutatis mutandis.
    🔗
    To see that \(R[x]\) is a ring, we need to check all eight axioms. Before we do that, recall in Proposition 2.2.3 that we have already seen polynomial addition is commutative provided the addition of coefficients is commutative. That proof therefore carries over mutatis mutandis.
    🔗
    We check associativity. Take \(p(x),q(x),r(x)\in R[x]\) and, by extending by 0, we can assume
    \begin{align*} p(x)\amp= a_0+a_1x+\cdots +a_nx^n \\ q(x)\amp= b_0+b_1x+\cdots +b_nx^n \\ r(x)\amp= c_0+r_1x+\cdots +r_nx^n \end{align*}
    where \(a_i,b_i,c_i\in R\) for \(i=0,\ldots,n\text{.}\) Then the coefficient of \(x^i\) in \(p(x)+(q(x)+r(x))\) is \(a_i+(b_i+c_i)\text{.}\) The coefficient of \(x^i\) in \((p(x)+q(x)) + r(x)\) is \((a_i+b_i)+c_i\text{.}\) Since \(R\) is a ring and addition is associative in \(R\) we have \(a_i+(b_i+c_i) = (a_i+b_i) + c_i\text{.}\) Since the coefficients of \(p(x)+(q(x)+r(x))\) and \((p(x)+q(x)) + r(x)\) are equal, they are equal polynomials.
    🔗
    We claim \(z(x) = 0_R\) is the additive identity. Take \(p(x)\in R[x]\) and write
    \begin{equation*} p(x)=a_0+a_1x+\cdots +a_nx^n. \end{equation*}
    Then extend \(z(x)\) by zero:
    \begin{equation*} z(x) = 0_R + 0_Rx+\cdots + 0_Rx^n. \end{equation*}
    The sum is then:
    \begin{align*} z(x)+p(x)\amp= (0_R+a_0) + (0_R+a_1)x+\cdots + (0_R+a_n)x^n \\ \amp= a_0 + a_1x+\cdots + a_nx^n \\ \amp= p(x) \end{align*}
    as \(0_R\) is the additive identity in \(R\) so that \(0_R+a_i = a_i\) for all \(i\text{.}\) Similarly, \(p(x) + z(x) = p(x)\text{.}\)
    🔗
    For additive inverses, suppose \(p(x)\) is as before. Then define \(-p(x)\in R[x]\) by: \(-p(x) = (-a_0) + (-a_1)x + \cdots + (-a_n)x^n\) where \(-a_i\) is the additive inverse of the coefficient \(a_i\) in \(p(x)\text{.}\) Then
    \begin{align*} \amp p(x)+(-p)(x)\\ \amp= (a_0+(-a_0)) + (a_1+(-a_1))x + \cdots + (a_n+(-a_n))x^n \\ \amp= 0_R + 0_Rx + \cdots + 0_Rx^n \\ \amp= z(x) \end{align*}
    Similarly \((-p)(x)+p(x) = z(x)\text{.}\)
    🔗
    The proof that multiplication is associative is particularly notationally dense and we leave it as an exercise. For the multiplicative identity, set \(i(x) = 1_R\text{.}\) Take a polynomial \(p(x)\) as before. We extend \(i(x)\) by zero via:
    \begin{equation*} i(x) = b_0+\cdots +b_nx^n \end{equation*}
    where \(b_0=1_R\) and \(b_i=0_R\) for \(i>0\text{.}\) Then the product
    \begin{equation*} p(x)\cdot i(x)= c_0+c_1x+\cdots +c_nx^n \end{equation*}
    where
    \begin{equation*} c_i = a_0b_i+\cdots + a_ib_0. \end{equation*}
    However, \(b_j=0\) for \(j>0\) so for \(k\neq i\) we have \(a_kb_{i-k}=0_R\) by Exercise???? In particular, \(c_i = a_ib_0 = a_i\cdot 1_R = a_i\text{.}\) Since the coefficients of \(p(x)\cdot i(x)\) and \(p(x)\) are equal, the polynomials are equal: \(p(x)\cdot i(x) = p(x)\text{.}\) Similarly \(i(x)\cdot p(x)= p(x)\text{.}\)
    🔗
    The remaining two proofs are left- and right-distributive properties. As they are similar, we only prove the left-distributive property. TBD.
    🔗
    🔗
    The preceeding theorem says that whenever we have a ring, polynomials with coefficients in that ring again form a ring. In particular, if \(R\) is a ring, then so is \(R[x]\text{,}\) and hence so is \((R[x])[y]\) and so on. This leads us to the corollary:
    🔗

    Corollary 2.2.14.

    Let \(R\) be a (commutative) ring and \(x_1,\ldots,x_n\) be indeterminates. Then the set of polynomials in \(x_1,\ldots,x_n\) with coefficients in \(R\text{,}\) \(R[x_1,\ldots,x_n]\text{,}\) is a (commutative) ring.
    🔗
    🔗

    Proof.

    We proceed by induction. The base case is \(n=1\text{.}\) This is the content of Theorem 2.2.13.
    🔗
    For the inductive step, suppose \(R[x_1,\ldots,x_n]\) is a (commutative) ring. By Theorem 2.2.13, \(R[x_1,\ldots,x_n][x_{n+1}]\) is again a (commutative) ring. But the latter is the \(R[x_1,\ldots,x_{n+1}]\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗
    We now have some great examples of commutative rings. Namely polynomial rings in some number of variables over any of \(\Z,\Q,\R,\) or \(\C\text{.}\) As we have yet to see an example of a non-commutative ring we remedy this now.
    🔗

    Example 2.2.15. Square Matrices.

    Consider the set \(M_2(\R)\) of square \(2\times 2\) matrices over \(\R\) (or whatever ring or field you like to work with). We recall matrix addition and multiplication:
    \begin{equation*} \begin{bmatrix}a_1 \amp b_1 \\ c_1 \amp d_1 \end{bmatrix} + \begin{bmatrix}a_2 \amp b_2 \\ c_2 \amp d_2 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix}(a_1+a_2) \amp (b_1+b_2) \\ (c_1+c_2) \amp (d_1+d_2) \end{bmatrix} \end{equation*}
    and
    \begin{equation*} \begin{bmatrix}a_1 \amp b_1 \\ c_1 \amp d_1 \end{bmatrix} \cdot \begin{bmatrix}a_2 \amp b_2 \\ c_2 \amp d_2 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} a_1a_2+b_1c_2 \amp a_1b_2+b_1d_2 \\ c_1a_2+d_1c_2 \amp c_1b_2+d_1d_2 \end{bmatrix} \end{equation*}
    🔗
    In Exercise???? you will check this is a ring. We show that multiplication is not commutative. To that end, consider:
    \begin{equation*} A=\begin{bmatrix}1 \amp 1 \\ 1 \amp 1 \end{bmatrix}, B =\begin{bmatrix}0 \amp 1 \\ 0 \amp 0 \end{bmatrix}. \end{equation*}
    Then
    \begin{equation*} A\cdot B = \begin{bmatrix}0 \amp 1 \\ 0 \amp 1 \end{bmatrix}, B\cdot A = \begin{bmatrix}1 \amp 1 \\ 0 \amp 0 \end{bmatrix}. \end{equation*}
    In particular, \(A\cdot B\ne B\cdot A\) and so matrix multiplication is not commutative.
    🔗
    🔗
    In the previous example, we observed that matrix multiplication failed to be commutative for matrices with real entries. What about for entries in a general ring? If you review that example, you will notice the only reason the products did not agree is because \(0\ne 1\) in \(\R\text{.}\) The next example shows that the multiplicative identity and additive identity sometimes agree.
    🔗

    Example 2.2.16. The Zero Ring.

    Let \(R=\{0\}\text{.}\) That is, a set with one element. Define addition and multiplication by \(0+0 = 0\) and \(0\cdot 0 = 0\text{.}\) We claim that addition and multiplication define a ring structure on \(R\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Almost all of the checks are straightforward. For example, to check associativity of addition we only have one element so we just check \(0+(0+0) = (0+0)+0\text{.}\) But there is only one thing both sides can be: \(0\text{.}\) So addition is associative.
    🔗
    The only property that warrants inspection is the multiplicative identity. We claim that in this ring \(1_R = 0\text{.}\) Indeed, we need to show that for every \(r\in R = \{0\}\) that \(0\cdot r= r\text{.}\) But there’s only element to check this on: \(r=0\) where it is true.
    🔗
    We conclude that the zero ring is a ring!
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    Subsection 2.2.3 Subrings: New Rings from Old

    Definition 2.2.17.

    Let \((R,+,\cdot)\) be a ring and let \(S\subseteq R\text{.}\) If \(S\) is itself a ring under \(+\) and \(\cdot\text{,}\) we say \(S\) is a subring of \(R\text{.}\) In this case, \(R\) is often called an overring of \(S\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗
    The following theorem provides a easy-to-apply test to check if a given subset \(S\) of a ring \(R\) is in fact a subring of \(R\text{.}\)
    🔗

    Theorem 2.2.18. Subring Test.

    Let \(R\) be a ring and \(S\) a subset of \(R\text{.}\) Then \(S\) is a subring if and only if:
    1. \(1_R\in S\text{;}\)
      🔗
      🔗
    2. \(S\) is closed under multiplication; and
      🔗
      🔗
    3. \(S\) is closed under subtraction.
      🔗
      🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    Proof.

    Suppose \(1_R\in S\) and \(S\) is closed under multiplication and subtraction. We check each of the axioms in Definition 2.2.7. Since \(1_R\in S\) and \(S\) is closed under subtraction we have \(0_R = 1_R-1_R\in S\text{.}\) For any \(a\in S\text{,}\) we know that \(a\in R\) so that
    \begin{equation*} a+0_R = 0_R+a=a\text{ and }a\cdot 1_R = 1_R\cdot a = a. \end{equation*}
    Thus \(0_R\) and \(1_R\) are the additive and multiplicative identities, respectively, in \(S\text{.}\)
    🔗
    For any \(b\in S\text{,}\) we know that \(-b=0_R-b\in S\text{.}\) Thus \(S\) is closed under additive inverses. If \(a,b\in S\text{,}\) then \(a+b=a-(-b)\in S\) so that \(S\) is closed under addition.
    🔗
    For any triple of elements \(a,b,c\in S\text{,}\) we know \(a,b,c\in R\) so that \(a+(b+c) = (a+b)+c\) and \(a+b=b+a\text{.}\) Thus associativity and commutativity of addition are inherited from \(R\text{.}\) Similarly, \(a\cdot(b\cdot c) = (a\cdot b)\cdot c\text{,}\) \(a\cdot(b+c) = a\cdot b + a\cdot c\text{,}\) and \((a+b)\cdot c = a\cdot c + b\cdot c\text{.}\) That is, \(S\) inherits associativity of multiplication as well as the left- and right-distributive properties from \(R\text{.}\)
    🔗
    We conclude that \(S\) is a ring under the multiplication and addition from \(R\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗

    Example 2.2.19.

    In Example 2.1.15, we saw that \(\Q(\sqrt{-5})\) was a field and so it is a commutative ring. Let’s define a subset of this field by taking those elements with integral real and imaginary part:
    \begin{equation*} \Z[\sqrt{-5}] = \{a+b\sqrt{-5}\mid a,b\in\Z\}. \end{equation*}
    We claim that \(\Z[\sqrt{-5}]\) is a subring of \(\Q(\sqrt{-5})\text{.}\) To that end we apply the subring test. That is, \(1_{\Q(\sqrt{-5})}\in\Z[\sqrt{-5}]\text{,}\) it is closed under subtraction and multiplication.
    🔗
    We see that \(1+0\sqrt{-5}\in\Z[\sqrt{-5}]\) and so our subset has the multiplicative identity.
    🔗
    For closure under subtraction, take \(a+b\sqrt{-5}, c+d\sqrt{-5}\in\Z[\sqrt{-5}]\text{.}\) Then
    \begin{equation*} (a+b\sqrt{-5})-(c+d\sqrt{-5}) = (a-c) + (b-d)\sqrt{-5} \end{equation*}
    and \(a-c,b-d\in\Z\text{.}\) We conclude \(\Z[\sqrt{-5}]\) is closed under subtraction.
    🔗
    For closure under multiplication we compute:
    \begin{equation*} (a+b\sqrt{-5})\cdot (c+d\sqrt{-5}) = (ac+5bd) + (ad+bc)\sqrt{-5} \end{equation*}
    and again \(ac+5bd,ad+bc\in\Z\text{.}\)
    🔗
    By the Subring Test, we conclude \(\Z[\sqrt{-5}]\) is a subring of \(\Q(\sqrt{-5})\text{.}\) In particular, it is a ring.
    🔗

    Subsection 2.2.4 Units and Associates in a Ring

    Definition 2.2.20.

    Let \(R\) be a ring and let \(u\in R\) be nonzero. If there is a \(v\in R\) such that \(uv = vu = 1\text{,}\) we say \(u\) is unit of \(R\text{.}\) We denote the set of units of \(R\) by \(R^\times\text{.}\) We say \(x,y\in R\) are associates if there exists some \(u\in R^\times\) such that \(x = uy\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗

    Exploration 2.2.6.

    Explicitly describe the set \(\Z^\times\text{.}\) What are the associates of 7 in \(\Z\text{?}\)
    🔗
    🔗
    In other words, a unit in a ring is a nonzero element with a multiplicative inverse. The existence of units is the primary difference between fields and commutative rings: in a field, all nonzero elements are units, while in a commutative ring, no nonzero elements need be units, as Theorem 2.2.22 demonstrates.
    🔗

    Example 2.2.21.

    We describe the units in \(\Z[x]\text{.}\) Notice that for \(p(x)q(x)\in\Z[x]\text{,}\) of degrees \(m,n\) respectively, the leading coefficient of \(p(x)\cdot q(x)\) is
    \begin{equation*} c_{m+n} = a_0b_{m+n} +a_1b_{m+n-1}+\dots + a_nb_m. \end{equation*}
    But \(a_i = 0\) for \(i\ge \) and \(b_j=0\) for \(j\ge n\text{.}\) Using this observation, we can conclude that \(c_{m+n}=a_mb_n\text{,}\) see Exercise???? Since \(a_m,b_n\ne 0\) and the product of two non-zero integers remains non-zero, we conclude that \(c_{m+n}\ne 0\text{.}\) In particular,
    \begin{equation*} \deg(p\cdot q) = \deg(p)+\deg(q). \end{equation*}
    🔗
    Now suppose that \(p\cdot q = 1\) in \(\Z[x]\text{.}\) Then
    \begin{align*} 0\amp=\deg(1) \\ \amp=\deg(p\cdot q) \\ \amp=\deg(p) + \deg(q). \end{align*}
    Since \(\deg(p),\deg(q)\geq 0\text{,}\) the only way this can happen is if \(\deg(p) = \deg(q) = 0\) so that \(p,q\) are constant polynomials. Thus \(p,q\) are units in \(\Z\text{.}\) We conclude
    \begin{equation*} \Z[x]^\times = \Z^\times = \{\pm 1\}. \end{equation*}
    🔗
    🔗

    Theorem 2.2.22.

    A commutative ring \(R\) in which every nonzero element is a unit is a field.
    🔗
    🔗

    Proof.

    Compare the axioms for a commutative ring and a field. The only thing missing from the ring axioms is the existence of multiplicative inverses for nonzero elements.
